Tamoxifen is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) that acts as an estrogen antagonist in breast tissue while functioning as a partial agonist in bone and liver tissue. Classified alongside aromatase inhibitors in performance pharmacology for its role in estrogen management, tamoxifen works through a fundamentally different mechanism—it blocks estrogen receptors rather than reducing estrogen production. It is widely used for gynecomastia treatment and as a component of post-cycle therapy.

The half-life of tamoxifen is approximately 5 days, with its active metabolite endoxifen contributing to prolonged estrogen receptor antagonism. A default dose of 20 mg per day is standard for gynecomastia reversal and PCT protocols, though 10 mg daily may suffice for mild symptoms. For post-cycle therapy, typical protocols run 4–6 weeks at 20–40 mg daily to stimulate LH and FSH release and restore endogenous testosterone production.

Unlike aromatase inhibitors, tamoxifen does not reduce systemic estrogen levels, which helps preserve beneficial estrogenic effects on lipids, joints, and bone density. Side effects can include mood changes, visual disturbances, and increased risk of thromboembolic events at high doses or extended durations. Tamoxifen is taken orally and has a detection time of approximately 2 months in athletic drug testing panels.
