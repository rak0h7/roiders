## Aesthetic & Lifestyle Peptides

### AHK-Cu

**Mechanism of Action** Tripeptide copper complex (Ala-His-Lys) with high affinity for copper ions, similar to GHK-Cu but with distinct tissue distribution and receptor interactions. Stimulates collagen and elastin synthesis in dermal fibroblasts, promotes vascular endothelial growth, and has potent hair follicle-stimulating effects via upregulation of growth factors including VEGF and FGF.

**Primary Uses** Hair growth stimulation and scalp health when applied topically; skin quality and anti-aging support. Often combined with GHK-Cu in dermal formulations for complementary copper peptide coverage.

**Typical Dosage** 1-5 mg per day subcutaneously or applied topically to the scalp in a carrier solution.

**Side Effects** Similar to GHK-Cu; injection site discomfort and copper:zinc balance considerations with chronic use. Tadalafil


### Injectable GHK-Cu

**Mechanism of Action** Copper-binding peptide that promotes wound healing, increases collagen and elastin synthesis, and exerts anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

**Primary Uses** Skin rejuvenation, wrinkle reduction, improved hair thickness, and systemic anti-aging support.

**Typical Dosage** 2.5-10 mg per day subcutaneously or topical formulations; injections can be painful.

**Side Effects** Injection-site pain; important to supplement zinc to maintain a healthy copper:zinc balance. AHK-Cu / Backup Source


### Kisspeptin

**Mechanism of Action** Neuropeptide that acts on GPR54 (Kiss1R) receptors in the hypothalamus to potently stimulate GnRH release, driving LH and FSH secretion and downstream sex hormone production. Functions as a master regulator of the HPG axis and is a key mediator of puberty onset and reproductive function.

**Primary Uses** Testosterone and LH stimulation; enhancing sexual arousal and drive in both men and women via central HPG axis activation. Also being explored for fertility support and hypogonadism management.

**Typical Dosage** 50-100 mcg subcutaneously as needed for acute hormonal and libido effects; pulsatile dosing protocols used in fertility research.

**Side Effects** Very well tolerated in trial data; transient flushing and mild nausea reported. Does not suppress the HPG axis, so no post-use suppression concerns. ### MT-1 (Afamelanotide)

**Mechanism of Action** Selective MC1R agonist targeting melanocytes to increase eumelanin production with greater receptor selectivity than MT-2, reducing sexual and appetite-related effects.

**Primary Uses** Gradual, more natural tanning and photoprotection, especially in very fair or photosensitive individuals; FDA-approved as an implant for erythropoietic protoporphyria.

**Typical Dosage** Clinically, 16 mg subcutaneous implant every ~2 months; research protocols often mirror MT-2-style loading (500-1000 mcg daily) with 250 mcg weekly maintenance.

**Side Effects** Generally milder than MT-2; transient nausea, flushing, fatigue; can still darken existing moles and reveal latent pigmented lesions. Oxytocin Acetate / Oxytocin Nasal Kit


### MT-2 (Melanotan II)

**Mechanism of Action** Non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist that stimulates melanogenesis and also impacts receptors related to libido and appetite.

**Primary Uses** Rapid skin tanning; libido enhancement is a strong secondary effect.

**Typical Dosage** Loading phase 500-1000 mcg daily; once desired tan is reached, ~250 mcg weekly for maintenance.

**Side Effects** Nausea, facial flushing, spontaneous erections, darkening of moles/freckles, and potential appearance of new pigmented lesions.


### Oxytocin Acetate / Oxytocin Nasal Kit

**Mechanism of Action** Endogenous nonapeptide hormone and neuromodulator that acts on oxytocin receptors throughout the brain and periphery. Centrally, modulates social bonding, trust, empathy, and stress response via limbic and hypothalamic circuits. Peripherally involved in smooth muscle contraction, lactation, and cardiovascular tone.

**Primary Uses** Social anxiety reduction, enhanced social bonding and trust, stress modulation, and as an adjunct in romantic/sexual contexts. Also used in research for autism spectrum and PTSD models.

**Typical Dosage** 10-40 IU intranasally as needed; subcutaneous injection protocols use 1-2 mg.

**Side Effects** Generally mild; possible mild nausea, headache, or paradoxical anxiety in some individuals with high baseline cortisol. Receptor desensitization with chronic high-dose use is a consideration. Kisspeptin


### PT-141 (Bremelanotide)

**Mechanism of Action** Melanocortin receptor agonist acting centrally to increase sexual desire; not dependent on vascular mechanisms like PDE5 inhibitors.

**Primary Uses** Treatment of low libido in both men and women.

**Typical Dosage** 250-1000 mcg subcutaneously a few hours before sexual activity.

**Side Effects** Very common nausea, flushing, possible increased blood pressure; high frequency use risks tolerance and potential long-term sexual anhedonia, so use should be infrequent. ## Ancillaries for STEROID USE

### Empagliflozin (25mg)

**Mechanism of Action** SGLT2 inhibitor, blocks renal glucose reabsorption, causing glucosuria. Beyond glycemic effects, produces natriuresis (sodium/fluid excretion), reduces preload/afterload, and has direct cardioprotective and nephroprotective effects independent of glucose lowering.

**Typical Dosage** 25mg daily.

**AAS Context** Highly relevant on cycles involving hepatic insulin resistance (trenbolone especially). Provides mild blood pressure reduction via natriuresis, offloads cardiac preload, and partially counters the insulin resistance and visceral fluid retention AAS induces. The cardioprotective data from EMPA-REG makes it arguably the most evidence-backed compound on this list for long-term cardiac harm reduction in AAS users.

**Side Effects** Genital yeast infections(rare), mild polyuria, rare euglycemic DKA risk (low in non-diabetics but real). HCTZ (25mg)


### Eplerenone (25mg)

**Mechanism of Action** Selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, blocks aldosterone at the MR receptor, reducing sodium retention and potassium excretion. More selective than spironolactone with negligible progesterone/androgen receptor activity.

**Typical Dosage** 25–50mg daily. Often 25mg is sufficient alongside telmisartan.

**AAS Context** Directly targets aldosterone-mediated fluid retention common on aromatizing compounds and GH. Preferred over spironolactone on AAS cycles because spironolactone's anti-androgenic activity interferes with the cycle. Also has direct cardiac antifibrotic effects relevant to AAS-induced myocardial fibrosis. ### Ezetimibe (10mg)

**Mechanism of Action** Inhibits NPC1L1 cholesterol transporter in the intestinal brush border, reducing dietary and biliary cholesterol absorption by ~50%.

**Typical Dosage** 10mg daily.

**AAS Context** AAS-induced dyslipidemia is primarily driven by hepatic lipase upregulation crushing HDL and elevating LDL, ezetimibe addresses the LDL side without the hepatotoxic burden of statins, making it preferable on oral AAS cycles where liver stress is already elevated.

**Side Effects** Minimal. Occasional GI discomfort. No hepatotoxicity concern. Rosuvastatin (10mg)


### HCTZ (25mg)

**Mechanism of Action** Thiazide diuretic, inhibits NCC cotransporter in the distal convoluted tubule, promoting sodium and water excretion.

**Typical Dosage** 25mg daily.

**AAS Context** Used for AAS-induced water retention and hypertension. Blunter and less potassium-sparing than eplerenone, making it less ideal as a standalone on AAS cycles where aldosterone-driven retention is the primary issue. More useful as an adjunct when BP remains elevated despite ARB and beta blocker.

**Side Effects** Hypokalemia (significant concern on AAS — monitor electrolytes), hyperuricemia, mild glucose dysregulation. Stacking with eplerenone partially offsets the potassium loss. Eplerenone (25mg)

### ACD856

**Mechanism of Action** Positive allosteric modulator of TrkA (the high-affinity NGF receptor), potentiating nerve growth factor signaling without directly binding the orthosteric NGF site. This sensitizes TrkA to endogenous NGF, driving cholinergic neuron survival, basal forebrain acetylcholine synthesis, and synaptic plasticity in memory-relevant circuits.

### Adamax

**Mechanism of Action** Adamax (also known as Adamantyl-G) is an adamantane-derived BDNF mimetic that activates TrkB receptors, the primary signaling receptor for BDNF. This drives downstream neuroplasticity cascades including MAPK/ERK and PI3K/Akt pathways, promoting synaptogenesis, dendritic spine density, and long-term potentiation. Structurally distinct from other nootropics given the adamantane scaffold which improves CNS penetration.

**Primary Uses** Neuroplasticity enhancement, memory consolidation, and cognitive drive. Particularly interesting for stacking with other TrkB-targeting compounds for synergistic neuroplasticity effects.

**Typical Dosage** Research protocols generally suggest 5-25 mg orally or sublingually, a few times per week given limited tolerance data.

### Bromantane

### Pregabalin (Lyrica)

**Mechanism of Action** Binds to the α2δ subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels in the CNS, reducing presynaptic calcium influx and thereby dampening the release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, norepinephrine, substance P). **Primary Uses** Clinically indicated for generalized anxiety disorder, neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, and adjunct epilepsy treatment. Recreationally used for its pronounced euphoria and anxiolysis, which hits harder and faster than its predecessor gabapentin.

## Weight Loss Peptides (GLP-1 & Multi-Agonists)

### Cagrilintide

**Mechanism of Action** Long-acting amylin analog; amylin is co-secreted with insulin and reduces food intake while slowing gastric emptying.

**Primary Uses** Weight loss, often in combination with GLP-1 agonists (e.g., cagrilintide + semaglutide).

**Typical Dosage** Commonly started around 0.25 mg three times per week, titrating up.

**Side Effects** Similar GI profile to GLP-1 drugs (nausea, bloating, constipation). Survodutide


### GLP-4 Metatrutide

**Mechanism of Action** Next-generation multi-receptor agonist targeting GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors simultaneously, similar in concept to retatrutide but with a distinct molecular structure. Designed to maximize weight loss efficacy through complementary receptor engagement driving satiety, insulin secretion, and energy expenditure simultaneously.

**Primary Uses** Obesity and metabolic syndrome; positioned as one of the most potent weight loss compounds currently in development.

**Typical Dosage** Protocols are still being established given early-stage development; weekly subcutaneous injection with gradual titration.

**Side Effects** Expected GLP-1 class GI profile; glucagon component may add night sweats and increased heart rate similar to retatrutide.


### Mazdutide

**Mechanism of Action** Dual agonist at GLP-1R and glucagon receptor.

**Primary Uses** Obesity, T2D, and MAFLD; already approved in China under the brand name Xinermei, co-developed globally with Eli Lilly.

**Typical Dosage** Once-weekly subcutaneous injection, 4 mg and 6 mg doses studied in Phase 3, with higher doses up to 16 mg explored in Phase 1.

**Side Effects** Standard GLP-1 GI profile. In a head-to-head trial vs semaglutide in T2D patients, mazdutide outperformed on both weight loss and blood sugar control. More niche drug I tend to only recommend Mazdu or Survo to people when other options fail at controlling hunger. Tesofensine


### Orforglipron (Oral GLP-1 Sema)

**Mechanism of Action** A non-peptide small molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist that can be administered orally. Unlike peptide-based GLP-1 agents that require subcutaneous injection, orforglipron is a small molecule that binds the GLP-1 receptor with high affinity and does not require co-administration with absorption enhancers.

**Primary Uses** Weight loss and glycemic control with the convenience of oral dosing rather than injectable administration.

**Typical Dosage** 3-36 mg orally once daily in clinical trials, with common research protocols starting at the lower end and titrating.

**Side Effects** Standard GLP-1 GI profile (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea); generally well tolerated with titration. GLP-4 Metatrutide


### Retatrutide

**Mechanism of Action** Triple agonist at GIP, GLP-1, and glucagon receptors; glucagon agonism increases energy expenditure and fat oxidation.

**Primary Uses** Extremely potent weight loss agent in development, producing very high percentage body weight reductions in early trials.

**Typical Dosage** Early strategies often start around 0.25 mg three times per week and titrate upward to manage tolerability.

**Side Effects** Strong GLP-1-like GI issues (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), potential increased heart rate due to glucagon action, and night sweats from elevated metabolic rate.


### Semaglutide

**Mechanism of Action** GLP-1 receptor agonist that slows gastric emptying, increases glucose-dependent insulin secretion, and powerfully suppresses appetite centrally.

**Primary Uses** Significant and sustained weight loss; also used for glycemic control in type 2 diabetes.

**Typical Dosage** Start at 0.25 mg per week and titrate over months to 1.0-2.4 mg per week maintenance.

**Side Effects** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, GI cramping; possible gallbladder issues and rare pancreatitis. Tirzapeptide


### Survodutide

**Mechanism of Action** Dual glucagon/GLP-1 receptor agonist developed by Boehringer Ingelheim/Zealand Pharma.

**Primary Uses** Obesity and MASH (metabolic fatty liver disease).

**Typical Dosage** Once-weekly subcutaneous injection, with Phase 2 trials running up to 4.8 mg/week.

**Side Effects** Standard GLP-1 class GI profile. Phase 2 trial data showed patients on 4.8 mg lost an average of 18.7% body weight over 46 weeks, with up to 40% of participants losing at least 20%. Mazdutide


### Tesofensine

**Mechanism of Action** Triple monoamine reuptake inhibitor that blocks reuptake of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin simultaneously. Reduces appetite centrally via monoamine elevation and increases metabolic rate through sympathomimetic activity. Originally developed for Parkinson's and Alzheimer's before its weight loss effects were identified in trials.

**Primary Uses** Potent appetite suppression and weight loss, particularly for individuals who have plateaued on GLP-1-class agents. More stimulant-like in character than GLP-1 based options.

**Typical Dosage** 0.25-0.5 mg orally once daily.

**Side Effects** Increased heart rate, dry mouth, insomnia, nausea, and constipation. Shares side effect overlap with stimulant-class compounds due to its monoamine mechanism. Blood pressure monitoring recommended. Orforglipron (Oral GLP-1 Sema)


### Tirzapeptide

**Mechanism of Action** Dual GIP and GLP-1 receptor agonist, enhancing both insulin secretion and satiety with greater weight-loss efficacy than semaglutide in trials.

**Primary Uses** Superior weight loss compared to GLP-1 monotherapy, plus robust blood sugar control in type 2 diabetics.

**Typical Dosage** Starts at 2.5 mg weekly, titrating up (5, 7.5, 10, or 15 mg weekly).

**Side Effects** Similar GI profile to semaglutide, often more pronounced at higher doses.
